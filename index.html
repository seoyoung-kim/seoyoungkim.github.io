a Ph.D. candidate in Marketing at the Terry College of Business, University of Georgia. I investigate paradigm shifts in the marketplace and their influence on firm decisions and business stakeholders' responses.
















ABOUT ME


As a theoretical/empirical researcher, I am interested in how market stakeholders — consumers, firms, and the public — respond to & learn from paradigmatic changes in the marketplace.

To investigate such dynamic relationships under uncertainty, I am studying a variety of substantive issues such as digital transformation, brand activism, or distribution channel evolution leveraging various methodologies such as applied econometrics, content analysis, network analysis, and statistical machine learning.

RESEARCH


WORK UNDER REVIEW

“Going Digital to Conform and Perform: Learning Logics Underpinning Digital Advertising Spending,” with Sundar Bharadwaj, under review at the Journal of Marketing Research.

Despite a rich understanding of how and when digital advertising contributes to firm performance, we know less about the antecedents of digital advertising spending. Drawing on the strategic change perspective, we delineate five learning logics, motivated by either conformance or performance, which explain how firms determine their digital advertising spending. A panel vector autoregression analysis complemented by a rolling hierarchical Bayesian regression approach and text analysis provides empirical evidence corroborating our theory that learning from an array of stakeholders – customers of a focal firm, peers in an industry, general consumers in the marketplace, and society – affects firms’ investment in digital advertising. In general, conformance-oriented learning logics play a more important role than performance-related learning logics in explaining digital advertising spending, which provides evidence for potential inefficiency in digital advertising spending decisions. Moreover, firms’ resource constraint perception and slack resource moderate the extent to which each learning logic explains a firm’s digital advertising spending. A non-parametric multivariate joint-trajectory clustering approach finds that firms with a learning style regarding digital advertising spending that is responsive to consumer reactions financially outperform both firms that seek to learn from multiple learning sources and firms that are indifferent to learning.

“A Meta-Analysis of Multichannel Distribution Effectiveness,” with Peng Zhang and Anindita Chakravarty, under the 2nd round review at the Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science.

This study is a meta-analysis based on 518 coefficient estimates of the relationship between multichannel distribution and firm performance. Integrating prior multichannel distribution literature, the authors investigate marketing-mix elements as well as customer and market attributes that influence multichannel distribution effectiveness. The analysis that accounts for firm, data and model attributes of the sampled studies reveals that firms’ multichannel distribution effectiveness is generally higher when they implement targeted communication, execute promotion-oriented communication across distribution channels, and maintain product prices consistent across distribution channels. Several marketing-mix elements’ effects are contingent on customer familiarity and market competitiveness. Further, we find that specific sequences of channel addition in distribution channel portfolios as well as particular channel assortments in distribution portfolios are more effective than others. Finally, we find that multichannel distribution effectiveness estimates in the literature are sensitive to the metric of firm performance as well as to the use of endogeneity corrections.

TEACHING


TEACHING STATEMENT

“Tell me and I forget. Teach me and I remember. Involve me and I learn.” — Benjamin Franklin

At the core of my pedagogical philosophy is the question, “Is it relevant enough to students?” I consider teaching a process in which I help students internalize new knowledge by making their learning be more relevant to them. My approach to teaching builds on my scholarly perspective of seeing marketing as a lens that allows us to better understand what is happening in our daily lives.

With my passion for marketing driving major parts of my life, marketing cannot be more relevant to me. However, I understand it may not be the case for most students taking my course. Thus, when teaching marketing to students, I strive to motivate them to realize the beauty of marketing and relate marketing to their lives as much as I do.
